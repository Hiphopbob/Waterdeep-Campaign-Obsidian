# “Shadow Between Waystones”

I was not born in a city of stone. My first memories are of canvas snapping in the wind and the creak of wagon wheels along the Trade Way. My people traveled between settlements north and south of [[The Sword Coast]], carrying silks, spices, timber, iron, and sometimes secrets. I learned early that the difference between a successful journey and a shallow grave is whether someone sees danger first.

That someone became me.

I took naturally to the high ground — wagon tops, rocky outcrops, tree limbs overlooking the road. While others haggled, I watched. While others slept, I listened. Bandits prefer the unprepared, monsters prefer the slow, and both prefer the blind. A steady aim and a quiet breath ended many threats before they reached the caravan line. I did not relish the killing, but I respected it. A clean shot meant fewer widows and fewer broken wagons.

Over time I was hired beyond my own caravan. Merchants began requesting me by description — “the Tabaxi with the long sight.” Some contracts were simple protection between towns. Others required a more… deliberate solution. A gang leader who had been targeting supply lines. A highwayman who would not be dissuaded. I told myself the same thing each time: remove the threat, protect the road, keep trade flowing. The hunt is not cruelty when it prevents greater harm.

In recent years my routes brought me frequently to [[Waterdeep]]. At first it was just another stop — loud, wealthy, full of too many smells and too little sky. But I learned its gates, its Dock Ward alleys, its caravan yards and counting houses. I learned which taverns pay guards fairly and which merchants delay coin. I learned that in Waterdeep, danger wears silk as often as it carries a blade.

My last contract was meant to be simple: escort a shipment north, return south with a new client already secured. The first leg went smoothly. The second fell apart before it began. My prospective employer vanished from negotiations — bought out, arrested, intimidated, or worse. No explanation. No pay. No follow-up work.

So now I remain in Waterdeep longer than intended.

I am not destitute, but neither am I wealthy. Coin runs thin when wagons stop moving. I could take to the road again, find another caravan, start over elsewhere. But that would mean rebuilding contacts from scratch. Here, I already have a foothold. I know the trade districts. I know the routes in and out. I know there is money in this city — more money than passes openly through any caravan train.

I am not a hero, nor am I a villain. I am a professional. I work for coin, but I follow my own code.

I do not harm beasts without reason. Animals kill to eat or defend; I understand them. Cruelty toward them tells me all I need to know about a person. I do not betray those who fight beside me. A hunting pack that turns on itself starves. And I do not take contracts that target the defenseless. There is always risk on the road — that is understood. But exploitation is something else entirely.

The laws of cities bend. So do I, when necessary. I have guarded caravans for merchants who underpay their laborers and I have removed threats whose deaths were quietly convenient for powerful people. I am not blind to the grey of it. But I choose my work carefully, and once chosen, I see it done cleanly.

If Waterdeep offers opportunity — legitimate or otherwise — I will seize it. Not recklessly. Not foolishly. With patience. With allies. With planning.

A lone hunter survives a season. A pack builds a future.

For now, my crossbow is oiled, my purse is light, and this city hums with possibility. I intend to listen.
